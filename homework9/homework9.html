Homework 9 — Interpretations of probability & measure-theoretic foundations

Classical / frequentist / Bayesian / geometric interpretations, the Kolmogorov axioms, measure theory link, and axiomatic derivations.

1 — Main interpretations of probability

Classical — probability as ratio of favourable outcomes to equally possible outcomes (Laplace). Works for symmetric problems (fair dice), but fails when outcomes are not equipossible.

Frequentist — probability as long-run relative frequency of an event in repeated independent trials. Natural link to experiments and the Law of Large Numbers.

Bayesian — probability as a degree of belief, updated with evidence via Bayes' rule. Useful for incorporating prior knowledge; subjective but coherent with axioms.

Geometric — probability defined by ratios of measures (lengths/areas/volumes) — e.g. Buffon's needle. This is an instance of measure-theoretic probability.

Why a formal axiomatic approach is helpful

The Kolmogorov axioms give a single mathematical framework that all interpretations must satisfy (if they are to be consistent). The axioms describe what a probability measure is without prescribing philosophical meaning — so frequentist, Bayesian, and geometric viewpoints are all realized as particular constructions of probability measures.

2 — Probability theory and measure theory

Measure theory formalizes probability on possibly uncountable spaces. Key objects:

  • Sample space \(\Omega\)
  • Sigma-algebra \(\mathcal{F}\) — events closed under complement and countable unions
  • Probability measure \(P:\mathcal{F}\to[0,1]\) satisfying Kolmogorov axioms
  • Random variable — measurable map \(X:(\Omega,\mathcal{F})\to(\mathbb{R},\mathcal{B})\)
\(\displaystyle E[X] = \int_\Omega X(\omega)\,dP(\omega)\)

Measure theory gives rigorous definitions for expectation, variance (via integrals), conditional probability (via conditional expectation and Radon–Nikodym derivatives), and convergence modes (almost sure, in probability, L^p). This resolves many pathological cases that naive calculus cannot handle.

3 — Axiomatic derivations

Subadditivity

From countable additivity: for any sequence of events \(A_1,A_2,\dots\) define disjoint sets

\(B_1=A_1,\quad B_2=A_2\setminus A_1,\quad B_3=A_3\setminus(A_1\cup A_2),\dots\)

Then \(\bigcup_i A_i = \bigsqcup_i B_i\), so

\(P\Big(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i\Big)=\sum_{i=1}^\infty P(B_i)\le \sum_{i=1}^\infty P(A_i).\)

Inclusion–Exclusion (finite)

Two sets:

\(P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B).\)

Three sets:

\(\begin{aligned} P(A\cup B\cup C) &= P(A)+P(B)+P(C) \\ &\ -\ [P(A\cap B)+P(A\cap C)+P(B\cap C)] \\ &\ +\ P(A\cap B\cap C). \end{aligned}\)

The general formula alternates signs and follows by repeated decomposition using additivity.

4 — Short extra notes (my additions)

On coherence: Bayesians insist on probability coherence (no Dutch book). Coherence is naturally encoded by the axioms: violating additivity leads to contradictions in betting models.

On practical modeling: choice of sigma-algebra matters: for continuous-time stochastic processes we must select sigma-algebras that allow us to measure path properties (right-continuous filtrations, etc.). This is why measure-theoretic vocabulary is not optional for advanced probability.
