Homework 4 — Law of Large Numbers

Definitions, formulas, and interactive simulations of relative frequency trajectories (Bernoulli trials). Use the controls to run experiments and visualize convergence.

Definition & explanation

Law of Large Numbers (LLN) — informally: as the number of independent, identically distributed (i.i.d.) trials increases, the sample average (or relative frequency) converges to the expected value.

Weak Law (WLLN)

Let \(X_1, X_2, \dots\) be i.i.d. random variables with finite expectation \(\mu = E[X_i]\). Define the sample mean \(\overline{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\). For every \(\varepsilon > 0\):

\(\lim_{n\to\infty} P(|\overline{X}_n - \mu| > \varepsilon) = 0.\)

Meaning: for large \(n\), \(\overline{X}_n\) is close to \(\mu\) with high probability.

Strong Law (SLLN)

Under the same assumptions, the strong law states:

\(P(\lim_{n\to\infty} \overline{X}_n = \mu) = 1.\)

This is a stronger statement: with probability 1 the sample mean converges to \(\mu\).

Example: Bernoulli trials

Let each \(X_i\) be a Bernoulli random variable with parameter \(p\) (success probability): \(X_i \in \{0,1\}\), \(P(X_i=1)=p\). Then \(\mu = p\), and the LLN implies the relative frequency of successes \(\frac{1}{n}\sum X_i\) converges to \(p\).

Why this matters in practice

  • LLN gives theoretical foundations for frequency-based estimates (e.g., estimating probability by frequency).
  • Simulations let you observe convergence behavior, variance of trajectories, and how many trials you need for a given accuracy.
