Homework 8 — Analogies & combinatorics (Binomial, Pascal, Fibonacci)

Discussion of relationships between the server/attacker model (HW7), Bernoulli LLN (HW4), binomial coefficients, Pascal's triangle and related combinatorial identities.

High-level analogy

Both Homework 4 (Bernoulli trials & LLN) and Homework 7 (weekly server security) are instances of repeated independent Bernoulli trials with a fixed success probability per trial: in HW4 a single Bernoulli(p) per trial, in HW7 a weekly Bernoulli(q) where \(q=1-(1-p)^m\). In both cases:

  • Counts of successes over \(n\) trials follow a Binomial(n, p') distribution (p'=p in HW4, p'=q in HW7).
  • Sample means (relative frequencies) converge to the true success probability by the LLN.
  • Empirical histograms of counts converge to the Binomial pmf as the number of simulated trajectories grows.

Binomial coefficients & Pascal's triangle

The binomial coefficient counts the number of ways to choose \(k\) successes among \(n\) trials:

\( {n \choose k} = \dfrac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} \)

Binomial pmf:

\( P(K=k) = {n \choose k} p'^k (1-p')^{n-k}. \)

Pascal's triangle arranges binomial coefficients. Each entry equals sum of the two above it, reflecting the recurrence:

\( {n \choose k} = {n-1 \choose k-1} + {n-1 \choose k}. \)

This combinatorial recurrence corresponds to the two possible outcomes of the last trial (success or failure), which is the same reasoning behind binomial probabilities.

Binomial expansion

The binomial theorem links coefficients to powers:

\( (x + y)^n = \sum_{k=0}^n {n \choose k} x^{k} y^{n-k}. \)

Setting \(x=p'\), \(y=1-p'\) recovers that the binomial pmf sums to 1 (normalization): \(\sum_k {n\choose k} p'^k (1-p')^{n-k} = 1\).

Connections to Fibonacci & combinatorial identities

While Fibonacci numbers have a different combinatorial origin, there are interesting cross-links:

  • A path-counting interpretation: certain restricted lattice paths counted by binomial-like coefficients produce Fibonacci numbers (e.g. compositions using steps 1 and 2).
  • Binomial sums generate identities that sometimes simplify to Fibonacci-like sequences. Example identity:
\( F_{n} = \sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} {n-1-k \choose k} \)

This counts ways to write n as sums of 1s and 2s — combinatorics bridging binomial coefficients and Fibonacci.

Mathematical properties & limit relations

Several important limits/approximations relate the binomial to other distributions:

  • Normal approximation: Binomial(n,p) ≈ Normal(np, np(1-p)) for large n (De Moivre–Laplace / CLT).
  • Poisson approximation: For n large and p small with λ = np fixed, Binomial(n,p) ≈ Poisson(λ).
  • These approximations explain how histogram shapes evolve as n and p change.

Practical interpretation & conclusions

In both HW4 and HW7 the same statistical machinery applies: counting independent trials → binomial pmf → relative frequencies → convergence by LLN. The HW7 mapping to ±1 random walk is a convenient visual: final S encodes the difference of successes and failures and translates directly to binomial counts.

Key takeaway for a report: show (1) the theoretical binomial pmf, (2) the empirical histogram from simulation, and (3) asymptotic approximations (normal) to discuss convergence. Mention combinatorial reasoning (binomial coefficients / Pascal) when explaining why probabilities are the way they are.
